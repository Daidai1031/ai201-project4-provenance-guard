# Provenance Guard Project Brief

Platforms where people share original creative work — writing, music, art — are facing a new challenge: how do you know whether what someone posted was made by them, or generated by AI and passed off as human? Not to police creativity, but to protect attribution, build trust, and give audiences the context they actually need.

In this project, you'll build **Provenance Guard**: a backend system that any creative sharing platform could plug into to classify submitted content, score confidence in that classification, surface a transparency label to users, and handle appeals from creators who believe they've been misclassified.

This is your most independent project so far. No planning template is provided — you'll design your system architecture from scratch before writing a single line of implementation code.

## 💡 Hints

A false positive — labeling a human's work as AI-generated — is worse than a false negative on a writing platform. Your confidence scoring and label design should reflect that asymmetry somewhere.

Your confidence score is a design decision before it is a technical one. Decide what you want `0.5` to mean to a user, then figure out how to achieve that.

The transparency label is a UX problem as much as a technical one. Show your label to someone who has not seen your project and ask what they understand from it before you finalize it.

Rate limiting numbers are not arbitrary. Think about realistic usage on a writing platform:

- How often does a single creator submit work?
- How would an adversary try to flood the system?
- How can you document your reasoning?

Your detection does not need to be perfect. Perfect AI detection is an unsolved problem. The system should acknowledge uncertainty honestly and give creators a path to appeal. That is the real engineering challenge here.

Before reaching for AI assistance, go back to your spec. If a feature is unclear, the answer is usually in a question you did not think through during Milestone 2.

## 📌 Note on Detection Signals

Your system needs at least **2 distinct signals**.

`Distinct` means they capture genuinely different properties of the text — not two versions of the same approach.

A strong default pairing:

### Signal 1: LLM-Based Classification with Groq

Ask the model to assess whether text reads as human-written or AI-generated.

This captures semantic and stylistic coherence holistically.

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

Measure statistical properties that may differ between human and AI writing, such as:

- Sentence length variance
- Type-token ratio, or vocabulary diversity
- Punctuation density
- Average sentence complexity

AI text tends to be more uniform. Human writing is often more variable.

This can be computed in pure Python.

### Why These Signals Work Together

These two signals are genuinely independent:

- One is semantic.
- One is structural.

That makes the combination more informative than either signal alone.

### Flask-Limiter Setup Note

Flask-Limiter `>= 3.x` requires a `storage_uri` parameter.

The simplest setup for local development uses in-memory storage:

```python
from flask_limiter import Limiter
from flask_limiter.util import get_remote_address

limiter = Limiter(
    get_remote_address,
    app=app,
    default_limits=[],
    storage_uri="memory://",
)
```

Apply the limit to your submit route:

```python
@app.route("/submit", methods=["POST"])
@limiter.limit("10 per minute;100 per day")
def submit():
    ...
```

### Rate-Limit Test Command

Run this in a new terminal window while your Flask server is running.

It sends 12 rapid requests, which exceeds the `10/minute` limit:

```bash
for i in $(seq 1 12); do
  curl -s -o /dev/null -w "%{http_code}\n" -X POST http://localhost:5000/submit \
    -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
    -d '{"text": "This is a test submission for rate limit testing purposes only.", "creator_id": "ratelimit-test"}'

```

You should see `200` responses for the first 10 requests and `429` responses after that.

Your repo root must include `planning.md`.

It should be:

- Written before implementation

## 3. `README.md`

Your `README.md` must include the following sections.

### Architecture Overview

Explain the path a submission takes from input to transparency label.

### Detection Signals

For each signal, explain:

- What it measures
- Why you chose it
- What it misses

### Confidence Scoring

Explain:

- How you combined signals into a score
- How you validated that the score is meaningful
- Two example submissions with noticeably different confidence scores:
  - One high-confidence case
  - One lower-confidence case

Show the actual scores.

### Transparency Label

Include a typed description of all three variants:

- High-confidence AI
- High-confidence human
- Uncertain

Show the exact text each one displays.

Screenshot or mockup is optional.

### Rate Limiting

Explain:

- The limits you chose
- Your reasoning for those specific values

### Known Limitations

Include at least one specific type of content your system would likely misclassify and why.

### Spec Reflection

Include:

- One way the spec helped you
- One way the implementation diverged from the spec and why

### AI Usage Section

Include at least 2 specific instances describing:

- What you directed the AI to do
- What it produced
- What you revised or overrode

- A few design decisions
